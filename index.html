Pre-IGCSE Biology Fun

Start your biology adventure! Learn about the amazing world of living things in a fun and easy way. Perfect for young scientists from around the world!

🌱

Amazing Plants

Discover how plants grow, make food from sunlight, and help us breathe!

Start Learning →
  • Plants make their own food using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide — this is called photosynthesis!
  • The tallest tree in the world is a coast redwood called Hyperion, standing at over 115 metres tall.
  • Some plants can actually move! The Venus flytrap snaps shut in less than a second to catch insects.
  • Plants release oxygen as a by-product of photosynthesis — that's the air we breathe!
  • The roots of a plant can grow incredibly deep to find water. Some desert plants have roots over 50 metres long.
🦋

Incredible Animals

Learn about different animals, how they live, eat, and survive in nature!

Start Learning →
  • A cheetah can run up to 112 km/h — that's faster than most cars on a highway!
  • Octopuses have three hearts and blue blood. Two hearts pump blood to the gills and one pumps it to the body.
  • Elephants are the only animals that can't jump, but they can communicate using vibrations through the ground.
  • The Arctic tern migrates from pole to pole each year — a round trip of about 70,000 km!
  • Dolphins sleep with one eye open and one half of their brain awake to watch for predators.
💪

Your Amazing Body

Explore how your body works — from your heart to your brain to your bones!

Start Learning →
  • Your heart beats about 100,000 times a day, pumping blood through 96,000 km of blood vessels!
  • Your brain uses about 20% of all the energy your body produces — it's the hungriest organ.
  • Babies are born with about 300 bones, but adults only have 206 because some fuse together as you grow.
  • Your nose can detect over 1 trillion different smells — way more than anyone imagined!
  • Red blood cells live for about 120 days. Your body makes about 2 million new ones every second.
🌍

Our Living Planet

See how all living things connect in ecosystems around the world!

Start Learning →
  • Rainforests cover only 6% of Earth's surface but contain more than half of all plant and animal species!
  • Coral reefs are called the “rainforests of the sea” because they support so much marine life.
  • A single teaspoon of soil can contain up to 1 billion bacteria — they help recycle nutrients for plants.
  • Ecosystems are all connected: if bees disappeared, about one-third of our food crops would be affected.
  • The Great Barrier Reef is so big it can be seen from space — it's over 2,300 km long!

🎉 Did You Know?

Your body has about 37 trillion cells! That's more than all the stars you can see in the night sky. Every single cell is working hard to keep you healthy and strong!

📋 Cell Biology Study Guide Topics

Click on a topic to learn more

1 Cell Structure & Organization ▼
Cells are the basic building blocks of all living organisms. There are two main types of cells:
  • Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) — no nucleus, smaller, simpler structure
  • Eukaryotic cells (animals, plants, fungi) — have a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles
  • Key organelles: nucleus (DNA), mitochondria (energy), ribosomes (proteins), cell membrane (barrier)
  • Plant cells also have: cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large vacuole
  • Levels of organization: cells → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism
2 Movement In & Out of Cells ▼
Substances move in and out of cells through several key processes:
  • Diffusion — movement of particles from high to low concentration (passive, no energy needed)
  • Osmosis — movement of water molecules through a partially permeable membrane
  • Active transport — movement against the concentration gradient (requires energy from respiration)
  • Exam tip: Always mention “concentration gradient” and “partially permeable membrane” in your answers
3 Biological Molecules ▼
Living organisms are built from four main groups of biological molecules:
  • Carbohydrates — provide energy (glucose, starch, glycogen, cellulose)
  • Proteins — made of amino acids; used for growth, repair, enzymes, and antibodies
  • Lipids (fats) — long-term energy storage, insulation, and cell membranes
  • Testing for molecules: Benedict's test (sugars), iodine test (starch), biuret test (proteins), ethanol test (lipids)
4 Enzymes & Their Functions ▼
Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions in living organisms:
  • Lock and key model — the substrate fits into the enzyme's active site like a key in a lock
  • Affected by: temperature (optimum ~37°C for humans), pH, and substrate concentration
  • Denaturation — at high temperatures or extreme pH, the active site changes shape and the enzyme stops working
  • Examples: amylase (breaks down starch), protease (breaks down proteins), lipase (breaks down fats)
5 Plant Nutrition & Photosynthesis ▼
Plants make their own food through photosynthesis:
  • Equation: carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen (light energy & chlorophyll needed)
  • Occurs in: chloroplasts, which contain the green pigment chlorophyll
  • Limiting factors: light intensity, CO₂ concentration, and temperature
  • Leaf adaptations: broad & flat (large surface area), thin (short diffusion distance), stomata (gas exchange), palisade cells packed with chloroplasts
6 Animal Nutrition & Digestion ▼
The digestive system breaks down food into small, soluble molecules that can be absorbed:
  • Mouth: mechanical digestion (teeth) and chemical digestion (amylase in saliva breaks down starch)
  • Stomach: produces hydrochloric acid and protease (pepsin) to break down proteins
  • Small intestine: where most digestion and absorption takes place; bile emulsifies fats
  • Large intestine: absorbs water; undigested food forms faeces
  • Villi: tiny finger-like projections that increase surface area for absorption in the small intestine

Bioluminescent Fungi: Nature's Glow Sticks

Deep in tropical forests, certain mushrooms emit an eerie green glow. Scientists believe this bioluminescence attracts insects that help spread spores.

FungiBioluminescenceEcology

How Does Bioluminescence Work?

Bioluminescence is the production of light by living organisms. In fungi, it's caused by a chemical reaction involving a molecule called luciferin and the enzyme luciferase. When luciferin reacts with oxygen in the presence of luciferase, energy is released as light.

Why Do Mushrooms Glow?

Scientists discovered that glowing mushrooms attract insects — especially beetles and flies — at night. These insects help spread the fungi's spores, acting as "fungal pollinators." It's an incredibly clever evolutionary adaptation!

Where Can You Find Them?

Over 80 species of bioluminescent fungi have been identified, most in tropical forests in Brazil, Japan, and Southeast Asia. The Mycena chlorophos is one of the most famous, glowing a brilliant green on the forest floor.

The Immortal Jellyfish: Turritopsis dohrnii

This tiny jellyfish can reverse its aging process and return to its polyp stage after reaching maturity. It's biologically immortal!

Marine BiologyRegenerationGenetics

What Makes It "Immortal"?

When stressed, injured, or old, Turritopsis dohrnii can undergo a process called transdifferentiation. Its cells essentially transform back into younger cell types, reverting the adult medusa back into a juvenile polyp. It's like a butterfly turning back into a caterpillar!

Can Humans Learn From This?

Scientists are studying this jellyfish to understand cellular rejuvenation. The process involves reprogramming cells — turning specialized adult cells back into stem-like cells. This research could have huge implications for medicine, aging, and regenerative therapy.

Fun Fact

Despite being "immortal," these jellyfish are only about 4.5 mm in diameter — smaller than a fingernail! They're native to the Mediterranean Sea but have spread to oceans worldwide.

CRISPR: The Gene-Editing Revolution

CRISPR-Cas9 allows scientists to edit DNA with unprecedented precision. From curing genetic diseases to creating drought-resistant crops.

GeneticsBiotechnologyCRISPR

What Is CRISPR?

CRISPR stands for Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats. It's a natural defence system that bacteria use to fight viruses. Scientists adapted it into a powerful gene-editing tool — like molecular "scissors" that can cut DNA at specific locations.

How Does It Work?

A guide RNA directs the Cas9 protein to the exact location on the DNA strand that needs to be edited. Cas9 then cuts the DNA, and scientists can remove, replace, or insert new genetic material. It's faster, cheaper, and more accurate than any previous method.

Real-World Applications

CRISPR is already being used to develop treatments for sickle cell disease, certain cancers, and hereditary blindness. In agriculture, it's creating crops that are more resistant to drought and disease. The possibilities are truly revolutionary!
